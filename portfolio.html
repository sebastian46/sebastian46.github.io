PORTFOLIO

While I may not have a Turing Award, I believe I have made decent contributions to Computer Science. As mentioned in my service section, I go back to my high-school during the winter break and talk to the seniors there. Not only do I inform them of the mistakes I made during my first year, but I also tell them more about Computer Engineering/Science. My school did not have a Computer Science club, since most people were not interested in such a math heavy subject. This leaves many people, like myself, uninformed and left wondering about what major they should go in to. After shedding some light onto it, some students decide they want to pursue a career in Computer Science. This means someone who previously would not have given this major a chance will now be making their own contributions to it in the future. Making small contributions is as important as making big contributions. Without all the little parts no one thinks about, the main parts will never work or advance.



Here are some more small contributions from my GitHub account. I will regularly update this page to show what projects I liked working on and completed. Expect to see more front-end related projects in the future. But for now, here are my current 3 favorite projects to which I have uploaded code for.







Industrial Instrumentation System

What: This project consists of 4 subsystems: power, GUI (physical), microcontroller, and web interface/database. I was in charge of the database/web interface subsystem. The whole system took in sensor inputs, converted the readings into numerical values, and displayed them on the GUI (physical) and web interface. For the web interface to receive information, the microconroller had to send values to the database (at least once per second).

How: The frontend for this project was made using Javascript (d3.js, HTML, CSS, Boostrap, JQuery). The backend was made using PHP and a local MySQL database.

Challenges and Solutions: This was the first time I worked in both the frontend and backend by myself. To solve this, I took a look at past projects where I'd done something similar and followed documentation found online.

Mountain Paths

What: This project takes a bird's-eye view of a mountainous region in grayscale, where white signifies the highest point(s) and black signifies the lowest point(s).

How: An output file will be given in .ppm form which highlights the easiest path (according to elevation change) in green. Other desirable paths will be highlighted in red.

Challenges, solutions, and possible improvements: This was one of my first coding projects. I had about 1 month of experience with coding before this. The biggest challenge was being overwhelmed and not knowing how to start. To solve this, I broke down the project into small sections (essentially Scrum Sprints). The program's runtime is not very good. There is nested for loops everywhere. One new approach I thought about a couple years after is using a pathfinding algorithm such as Dijkstra's algorithm.

IMDB

What: This project invovled storing all movies in a database (IMDB) and performing complex queries to get desired output.

How: The movies/shows (over 100,000) were stored in a PostgreSQL database and accessed with queries according to user specifiations.

My Part: My original part was to create an algorithm which outputs an actor who has performed in a movie every year from, for example, 2001-2010. However, I decided to also tackle one of the bonus questions which involved recommending a movie based on actor, year and genre. The actor could not be part of the movie in any way though.

Challenges and Solutions: One of the biggest technical issues was trying to optimize the runtime. My part would take over 10 minutes to run. To fix this, I decided to sacrifice the quality of recommendations. Instead of looking through all possible related films, I only chose about 20 to look at, and outputted 5.
